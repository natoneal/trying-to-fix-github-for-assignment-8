public art

user centered research

Research into how citizens interact with public art

You walk around your city all the time. What do you see? Do you see the city’s identity - its history, values, personalities, and stories? Public art is one of the primary avenues for giving physical form to a city’s identity, but community members often find that public art does not reflect this identity.

In our research, we discovered that residents value public art when it is relevant to their conception of the community’s identity. Based on these findings we developed pARTicipate, a tool to help create art that is relevant in this way.

Through our conversations with many local Pittsburgh artists and residents, using research methods including observe & intercept, think-aloud, speed dating, and experience prototyping, we discovered compelling insights.

Many citizens and artists posited that art should reflect culture. Interaction with public art increases the value of art to users and even minor involvement in the art creation process early on can generate and enhance community connections to the art. We spent the rest of the project exploring how to deepen individuals’ connections to their community through public art.

Using these insights, we developed a potential solution we call pARTicipate that involves an interactive survey to get data on what themes and types of art citizens think reflects the community. This information can be provided to artists when they create public art. Community members said they would feel they had tangibly contributed to the creation of public art if this type of solution was implemented.

Art that is relevant to citizens increases the livability of a city, which itself it a justification for the public expenditure on art. Through pARTicipate, artists can create this relevant art for citizens.

Team: Natalie Schade, Katie Johnstone, Marc Dubin, Edward Cho, Shan Wang
